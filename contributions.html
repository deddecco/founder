What is TCP/IP?

TCP stands for "Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol." Protocols are the sets of standards we use to correctly, safely, and quickly move information around the internet. TCP/IP ensures, unlike UDP, which is another protocol, that all the data gets from point A to point B losslessly. You might use UDP behind the scenes on a Zoom call-- your image might get grainy, or you could hear a few seconds of choppy audio, but most of the audio and video still makes it through to the other end, even if not perfectly--where speed takes the place of accuracy as teh primary concern. But when accuracy matters most, like in making sure all the contents of an email get to their destination, you will TCP. TCP keeps track of which packets have been sent and which ones have been received and can resend those which encountered issues along the way. It doesn't matter if packets come in out of order, because there will be processes at the destination that put the packets in the proper order,

According to a Crash Course Computer Science video, TCP does this primarily with two functionalities:

  • checksums
  • ack messages

Checksums-- also found in things like credit card numbers-- allow the data to validate itself. You probably can't guess a credit card number at random, so it would be quite hard to have illicit purchases on your account that way, precisely because the payment processor is looking for the sum of the digits of your card number, possibly mod something else, to yield a certain result. TCP uses checksums to make sure the data in the packet on the sending end hasn't changed by the time it gets to the receiving end. There is some formula for calculating the checksum of the data in the TCP packet, which is checked when the package is sent and recalculated when the package is received. If they match, the data got through without issues. If the check sum is right, your packet is accepted. If the checksum is right, you'll be allowed to check out from the online store. If not, something corrupted the package, and it can be requested again because the sequence in which packets are sent is known.

Acknowledgements work by sending extra messages across the network-- slowing down the transfer of information-- to let the sender know the receiver got their packet. You can send packet 1, get an ack, send packet 2, get an ack, send packet 3... get nothing... and then you know there was some kind of problem and packet 3 needs to be resent, That can either happen before you send packet 4, or when you're done sending however many packets you have, because one of the benefits of TCP is that it keeps track of the order of the reassembly of the packets.

According to PCMag, these acknowledgement messages take 3 steps:

  1. synchronize
  2. acknowledge the first packet
  3. acknowledge the acknowledgement

With this 3-way handshake protocol, both ends of a connection speaking the language of TCP/IP know they can hear each other. One message is sent to "synchronize," then a "synchronize acknowledge" message is sent in the opposite direction to acknowledge the first message, and finally a third message is sent in the original direction to acknowledge the second, and the connection is made.
