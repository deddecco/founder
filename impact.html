Vint Cerf: the Creator of TCP/IP

TCP/IP in the real world

We use TCP/IP every day as a backbone of the modern internet. The Erie Institute of Technology says that, "TCP and IP are two sides of the same coin. They each provide their own important functions for the delivery of data for the TCP/IP protocol. The TCP does the work to allow data to be received between two connected computers. IP finds the destination, or location, of the communicating computers, allowing the data to be transmitted between the two correct devices or internet applications." The delivery of this information happens in small chunks called "packets." The Gallery page has a diagram of a packet. Certain types of data can be transmitted in a way that some information loss-- like a picture or video getting slightly grainier-- is OK. Other types of data, like the text content of an email from a junior employee to their boss, need to be transmitted perfectly: everything arrives to the right receiver, from the right sender, without any damage to the data along the way. This video from NordVPN helps explain why someone would use TCP/IP.

For having invented TCP/IP, in 2005, Vint Cerf won the Turing Award given by the ACM-- considered in the industry to be as prestigious in computer science as a Nobel would be in a field that has one. The citation is available to read on the Photo Gallery page.

Every day, we make use of TCP/IP billions of times a day, and most of the time we aren't even aware that it is helping us-- to email a boss, to watch a YouTube video, to get directions to a new restaurant, and so much more.
